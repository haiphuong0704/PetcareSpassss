How to Prepare Your Dog for
Their First Boarding Stay

Linh Nguyen, Pet Wellness Specialist
May 10, 2026
5 min read

Leaving your dog at a boarding facility for the first time can feel nerve-wracking — for both of you. But with the right preparation, your pup can settle in quickly, feel safe, and maybe even enjoy the adventure. Here's everything you need to know before drop-off day.

Why Preparation Makes All the Difference

Dogs are creatures of habit. Sudden changes in environment, routine, and social interactions can trigger anxiety — especially in younger or more sensitive dogs. A little groundwork at home goes a long way toward helping your dog feel calm and confident when they arrive at the facility.

"A dog that feels secure in their environment will adapt to a new one far more gracefully than one who hasn't been prepared."

— Linh Nguyen, Pet Wellness Specialist at PetCare Spa

Visit the Facility First

Before the actual stay, schedule a short visit with your dog. Let them sniff around, meet the staff, and get comfortable with the sights, sounds and smells. Most quality boarding facilities like PetCare Spa welcome trial visits — they want your dog to feel at home too.

Health Records & Vaccinations

Most reputable boarding facilities require up-to-date vaccinations to keep all animals safe. Make sure you have the following ready before your dog's stay:

  • Rabies vaccination (required by law in most regions)
  • DHPP (distemper, hepatitis, parvovirus, parainfluenza)
  • Bordetella (kennel cough) — especially important for group stays
  • Flea and tick treatment within the last 30 days
💡
Pro Tip

Bring a physical copy of your dog's vaccination records as well as a digital backup on your phone. Some facilities won't accept dogs without documentation on arrival day.

Pack Comfort Items from Home

Familiar smells are incredibly calming for dogs. Bringing items from home can reduce separation anxiety significantly and help your dog settle faster into their new temporary space.

🎒 What to Pack for Your Dog
  • A worn t-shirt or blanket with your scent on it
  • Their regular food (to avoid digestive upsets from diet changes)
  • A favourite toy or chew (clearly labelled with their name)
  • Any medications with written dosage instructions
  • Emergency contact numbers including your vet

Stick to Their Routine as Long as Possible

In the days leading up to the boarding stay, keep your dog's feeding schedule, walk times and sleep routine as consistent as possible. A dog arriving at a facility already tired from disrupted sleep will be far more stressed than one that's well-rested.

On the morning of drop-off, keep your energy calm and upbeat. Dogs are incredibly attuned to their owners' emotions — if you're anxious, they'll feel it. A cheerful, matter-of-fact goodbye is much better than a lengthy, emotional farewell.

Socialisation Practice

If your dog hasn't spent much time around other dogs, it's worth arranging a few playdates or visits to a dog park beforehand. Boarding facilities often involve some level of group interaction, and a dog that's comfortable around peers will have a much better time.

What to Expect When You Pick Them Up

Don't be alarmed if your dog seems a little tired or subdued when you collect them — this is completely normal. They've had a full schedule of activity and stimulation. Some dogs sleep for an entire day after boarding. Others bounce off the walls with excitement. Both are normal responses.

  • Check for any scratches or minor injuries (usually harmless from play)
  • Rehydrate them with fresh water as soon as you're home
  • Give them space to decompress — avoid immediately introducing visitors
  • Expect some extra clinginess for a day or two — it's their way of reconnecting

With the right preparation, your dog's first boarding stay doesn't have to be stressful — it can be the start of a wonderful routine that gives you peace of mind every time you travel.
